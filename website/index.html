About

I study what AI systems actually do versus what we assume they do.

My work sits at the intersection of mechanistic interpretability and applied AI. I'm interested in measurement—and especially mismeasurement. How do we know what we think we know? What signals are we missing, and what noise are we mistaking for signal?

This question has followed me from a decade in data science (consulting, Marriott International) through building AI products (TerraSol, solar site analytics) to my current focus: understanding the internal structure of language models.

I believe the path to beneficial AI runs through genuine understanding—not just of model outputs, but of the computations that produce them. The ancients asked what it means to know something. We're now asking the same question of machines.

Research

Recent work in mechanistic interpretability and AI forecasting.

Fine-Tuning Entangles Epistemic Representations

Alignment training makes internal epistemic states harder to distinguish. RLHF/DPO roughly doubles the entanglement effect compared to SFT alone.

Key Findings

  • Probed hidden states across 8 models (4 families × base/instruct)
  • Fine-tuning entangles trained epistemic behaviors with genuine uncertainty
  • Entropy-based uncertainty estimation degrades after alignment across all models
Code

Self-Reference Circuits in Language Models

Investigating how LLMs process self-referential prompts. English-centric models compress self-reference circuits during instruction tuning; multilingual models preserve them.

Key Findings

  • English models (Llama, Mistral): 68-81% of layers reduce self-reference focus
  • Qwen (multilingual): treats self-reference as linguistically distinct circuit
  • Developed Role-Focus Coefficient (RFC) metric for measuring role-conditioning
Code

AGI Timeline Forecasting

Hierarchical Bayesian analysis combining compute scaling with task performance. Translation efficiency—not raw compute—emerges as the bottleneck.

Key Findings

  • Current frontier: ~22% of human-level on filtered subhuman tasks
  • Modern models show 43% less scaling efficiency vs GPT-3 era
  • LUCR decomposition separates scaling efficiency from translation efficiency
